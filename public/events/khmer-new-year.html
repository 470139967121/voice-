🏛️ What is Choul Chnam Thmey?

Choul Chnam Thmey (ចូលឆ្នាំថ្មី), meaning "entering the new year," is Cambodia's most celebrated holiday. Observed from April 13 to 16, it marks the end of the harvest season and the beginning of the Khmer solar calendar's new year.

Rooted in Theravada Buddhist tradition, the festival is a time for spiritual renewal, paying respect to elders, and gathering with family. Across Cambodia, temples overflow with offerings, homes are cleaned and decorated, and communities come together for traditional games, music, and feasting.

The celebration coincides with the end of the dry season, when farmers have completed their harvest and can take time to rest, celebrate, and prepare for the coming rains. It shares cultural connections with Songkran in Thailand, Pi Mai in Laos, and Thingyan in Myanmar — all marking the solar new year in April.

📿 The Three Days

Choul Chnam Thmey unfolds over three ceremonial days, each with distinct rituals and significance. A fourth day is sometimes observed as a continuation of the celebrations.

Maha Sangkran — មហាសង្ក្រាន្ត
Day 1 · April 13

The first day marks the arrival of the new celestial angel (Tevoda) who will watch over the world for the coming year. Families clean their homes thoroughly, prepare offerings of food and incense, and dress in new clothes. Homes and pagodas are decorated with colourful lights, and people gather at temples for prayers and blessings. In the evening, incense is lit to welcome the new Tevoda.

Virak Wanabat — វិរ:វ័នបត
Day 2 · April 14

The second day is dedicated to charity, generosity, and honouring elders. Families visit monasteries to offer food to monks and build sand hillocks (ភ្នំខ្សាច់) at temples — each grain of sand symbolizing the release of a sin. Younger family members pay respect to parents and grandparents by washing their hands and feet, seeking blessings for the year ahead.

Tngai Leang Sak — ថ្ងៃលើងសក
Day 3 · April 15–16

The final day is the most sacred. Buddhists bathe Buddha statues with perfumed water as a symbolic act of purification, and elders are bathed in the same tradition. Water is sprinkled on friends and neighbours as a blessing. Families share a celebratory meal, and the evening is filled with traditional music, dancing, and community games. In rural areas, celebrations can continue for several more days.

✨ Traditions & Customs

🏯

Temple Visits & Offerings

Devotees bring food, flowers, and incense to pagodas. Monks receive alms and chant prayers for prosperity. Many Cambodians spend the first morning at their local wat (temple).

💧

Water Ceremonies

Perfumed water is gently poured over elders' hands as a sign of respect and to receive their blessings. Buddha statues are ritually bathed. Water splashing between friends symbolizes washing away misfortune.

⛰️

Sand Hillocks (ភ្នំខ្សាច់)

Small mountains of sand are built at temples, each grain representing a bad deed released. Flags and flowers adorn the hillocks. This tradition symbolizes spiritual cleansing and merit-making.

🎮

Traditional Games

Chol Chhoung (scarf tossing between boys and girls), Leak Kanseng (hiding a towel in a circle), and tug of war are played in temple grounds and villages, bringing communities together in joyful competition.

🍜

Khmer Food

Num Ansom (sticky rice cakes wrapped in banana leaves), Kralan (bamboo sticky rice), and various curries are prepared days in advance. Families share these festive dishes together.

👗

New Clothes & Family

Wearing new clothes symbolizes a fresh start. Families gather from near and far — it's the biggest homecoming of the year. Children receive gifts and blessings from elders.

The Horse (មមី) symbolizes energy, freedom, and adventure in Khmer astrology. People born in Horse years are said to be lively, independent, and courageous. The Horse year encourages taking bold steps, pursuing new opportunities, and embracing change with confidence.
